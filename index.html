About Waimapu Sub-Catchment

The sub-catchment is 21 kilometres long and six kilometres wide, covering an area of 11,757 hectares. It includes 472 kilometres of stream margins with approximately 50 percent in pasture and over 30 percent in indigenous vegetation. Less than 10 percent of the catchment is in exotic forestry and there is increasing urban development in the lower catchment.

The catchment geology is dominated by volcanic rock with Mamaku and Waimakariri ignimbrites in the east and the older Waiteariki ignimbrite to the east. The primary waterways in the sub-catchment are the Waimapu and Waiorohi Rivers.

Drinking Water Supply

Tauranga's Drinking Water Supply comes partly from the Waimapu Sub-Catchment.Tauranga City Council provides all urban and rural-residential properties in Tauranga with constant, sustainable and high quality water.

The city water supply is treated using microfiltration technology at the Oropi and Joyce Road water processing plants. The water is filtered through very tiny holes and millions of tiny straws that remove bacteria like giardia, cryptosporidium, and dirt. Once filtered, the water is chlorinated to kill off any remaining contaminants. See more information on the Tauranga City water supply.

Water is important for everyone in the Bay of Plenty, whether it is used for drinking, gathering watercress, kayaking or fishing, irrigation for farming or industry. The Western Bay of Plenty area has the fastest-growing population in the region. It has been predicted that demand for water in the Western Bay will almost double between 2005 and 2055!

Waimapu Sub-Catchment Action Plan

This action plan provides an overview of the land types and land use in the Waimapu sub-catchment. It has have been informed by ground surveys and community consultation and has been used as a basis to set targets for land management work being carried out by Regional Council over the coming ten years.
