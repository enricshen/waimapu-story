Futureproof Your Land

Soil has been, and continues to be lost from the sub-catchment at moderate to high rates, especially where steep land is subject to cattle or deer grazing, or where earthworks (such as subdivision and roading) are not carefully managed. Soil loss into the waterways results in increased levels of sedimentation into our waterways and estuaries.

Sedimentation is the build-up or settling of sediment. Sediment is soil transported by wind or water. Sediment can be small particles of rock, shells, silt, mud, vegetation and other material. It has been measured that 1000 times more sediment comes from a bare patch of disturbed land (such as earthworks) than from a forested area. Less vegetation cover means more sediment will get into our waterways.

Water quality is also degraded by excess nutrients in streams from fertilisers, farm runoff and urine patch leaching. These and pollutants are generally unintentional by-products of activities such as farming and construction.

Priority 1

Regional Council is working with landowners to reduce sedimentation, from entering the water ways and estuary.

Priority 2

Regional Council is working with landowners to mitigate risks to water way and harbour water quality.

  • We do this by providing funding and practical advice to help landowners on:
    • Fencing and planting riparian margins to exclude stock and protect water quality.
    • Install stock stream crossings, such as bridges, to protect the water quality of streams.
    • Retire steep erodible land.
    • Protecting existing wetlands, native forest remnants and other areas of indigenous biodiversity.

As at 30 June 2016, 289.1 km out of a total of 472 km of water way margins has been fenced in the Waimapu sub-catchment.

Between 2012 and 2016, we have established 28 new management plans with landowners in the Waimpau sub-catchment whose properties were at risk from erosion. These plans have resulted in changes in land use resulting in 85.4 hectares where stock has been removed, new fencing installed, and native species have been replanted.
